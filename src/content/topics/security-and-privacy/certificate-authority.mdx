## In simple terms

Public-key cryptography solves eavesdropping — but how do you know the public key you received actually belongs to `bank.com` and not an attacker? A **Certificate Authority (CA)** answers this: it is an organisation that verifies the identity of a website or company and then signs a **certificate** binding their public key to their name. Your browser trusts a list of CAs; if a certificate is signed by one of them, you can trust the key.

## More detail

A **digital certificate** (X.509 format) contains:
- The subject's identity (`CN=*.bank.com` or an organisation name for EV certificates).
- The subject's **public key**.
- Validity period (not before / not after).
- The **issuer** (the CA that signed it).
- The **CA's digital signature** over all the above.

**Verification:** your browser verifies that the server's certificate is signed by a CA in its **trust store** (a list of ~150 root CAs pre-installed by the OS and browser vendors). If the chain of signatures leads back to a trusted root, the certificate is valid.

**Certificate chain:** Root CAs are kept offline for security. They sign **intermediate CAs**, which sign end-entity (leaf) certificates. A typical chain is: Root CA → Intermediate CA → Server certificate. Browsers verify the chain from leaf to a trusted root.

**Certificate types by validation level:**
- **DV (Domain Validation)** — CA verifies domain control only (DNS record or file on the server). Automated, free (Let's Encrypt). Gives the padlock but says nothing about the organisation.
- **OV (Organisation Validation)** — CA verifies legal entity. Takes days.
- **EV (Extended Validation)** — strict identity verification; used to show the company name in the browser bar (largely deprecated by browsers).

**Let's Encrypt** is an automated CA (ACME protocol) that issues free DV certificates with 90-day expiry, renewed automatically by tools like Certbot and Caddy. It has issued billions of certificates and driven HTTPS adoption from ~40% to ~90% of web traffic.

**Certificate Transparency (CT):** public append-only logs of all issued certificates. Browsers reject certificates not in at least two CT logs. This makes it impossible for a rogue CA to issue a certificate for `google.com` without it being detected within hours.

**Revocation:** if a private key is compromised, the certificate must be revoked via CRL (Certificate Revocation List) or OCSP (Online Certificate Status Protocol). CRL checking is largely abandoned by browsers (latency, privacy); OCSP Stapling (server pre-fetches its own OCSP response and staples it to the TLS handshake) is the modern approach.

## Why it matters

CAs are the trust infrastructure of the entire public web and most enterprise PKI. Without them, HTTPS would require pre-distributing public keys out-of-band — impractical at internet scale. Understanding CAs explains: why browsers show certificate errors, what a wildcard certificate covers, why certificate expiry causes outages, and why misissuance by a CA (e.g., Symantec's 2017 scandal) is so serious. Engineers who configure servers, manage internal PKI, or debug TLS errors need to understand the chain of trust.

## Real-world examples

- Let's Encrypt issues ~400 million active certificates for free; almost every small website uses it.
- DigiCert, Sectigo, and GlobalSign are commercial CAs used for code signing and EV certificates.
- Internal corporate CAs (often Windows Server CA) issue certificates for internal services, VPNs, and mTLS.
- Certificate Transparency logs (Google's Argon, Cloudflare's Nimbus) are monitored by companies like Facebook to detect rogue certificates for their domains.

## Common misconceptions

- **"HTTPS means the site is safe."** HTTPS means the connection is encrypted and the domain in the certificate matches the URL. It says nothing about whether the site is legitimate or the content is trustworthy.
- **"Free certificates are less secure."** DV certificates from Let's Encrypt provide the same cryptographic security as expensive certificates — the difference is identity verification, not key strength.
