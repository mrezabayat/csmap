## In simple terms

**Normalization** is a set of design rules for relational databases. You break wide tables into narrower, more focused ones so the same fact is stored in exactly one place. That way, updating a customer's address means updating one row, not hunting through every order they ever placed.

## More detail

Normalization is described as a series of **normal forms** (NF), each addressing a specific kind of redundancy:

- **1NF**: each cell holds a single atomic value; no repeating groups.
- **2NF**: in addition to 1NF, every non-key column depends on the *whole* primary key, not just part of it.
- **3NF**: every non-key column depends *only* on the key — no transitive dependencies via other non-key columns.
- **BCNF (Boyce-Codd)**: a stricter 3NF; every non-trivial functional dependency has a superkey on the left.
- Higher forms (4NF, 5NF, 6NF) address multi-valued and join dependencies; rarely needed in practice.

In practice, OLTP databases aim for 3NF / BCNF and back off only with reason.

**Denormalization** is the deliberate inverse: copy data across tables (or add a precomputed column) to speed up specific reads. Common in analytics, dashboards, and search indexes. Denormalize *after* measuring, never as a default.

## Why it matters

A well-normalised schema makes updates simple and prevents the database from telling itself contradictory stories. A denormalised schema can be much faster to read but easier to corrupt; the right balance depends on the workload.

## Real-world examples

- A spreadsheet with "customer name" repeated on every order row is 1NF but not 3NF. A schema with a `customers` table referenced by `customer_id` is 3NF.
- A data warehouse may store a denormalised "wide" table per business question because it is loaded once and read many times.
- A bug where customers see two different addresses for themselves is often a denormalisation that drifted out of sync.

- The 1973 paper that introduced 3NF is one of the most influential computer science papers ever; everything from PostgreSQL to Airtable still organises data on its principles.

## Common misconceptions

- **"Higher normal form is always better."** 3NF is usually the sweet spot. Higher forms can hurt performance without fixing real anomalies.
- **"Denormalisation is a smell."** It's a tool. In the right place, it is exactly right.
