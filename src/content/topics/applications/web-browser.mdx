## In simple terms

A web browser is the program you use to look at websites. It takes a URL, fetches the page, and turns the text it receives into the colourful, clickable thing on your screen.

## More detail

A modern browser is one of the most complex pieces of consumer software in the world. Inside, it has at least:

- A **networking stack** that speaks HTTP/1.1, HTTP/2, HTTP/3, TLS, DNS-over-HTTPS, WebSockets, etc.
- An **HTML parser** that builds a DOM tree from the bytes the server returned.
- A **CSS engine** that resolves styles and computes layout.
- A **JavaScript engine** (V8 in Chrome/Edge, SpiderMonkey in Firefox, JavaScriptCore in Safari) that runs page scripts.
- A **rendering pipeline** that turns the styled, scripted page into pixels on a GPU.
- A **sandbox** that isolates each site from your machine and from other sites.

The dominant engines are **Blink** (Chrome, Edge, Brave, Opera, Arc), **WebKit** (Safari), and **Gecko** (Firefox).

## Why it matters

The browser is the most important application platform of the last twenty years — it is how billions of people use software every day. It is also, in effect, an operating system in its own right: it sandboxes programs (web apps), manages permissions, talks to hardware, and runs continuously.

## Real-world examples

- Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge.
- An Electron app is a Chromium browser bundled with a Node.js runtime.
- Many "native" mobile apps embed a system web view to render parts of their UI.

- Chrome's V8 engine compiles JavaScript through three tiers (Ignition interpreter → SparkPlug baseline JIT → TurboFan optimizing JIT) — moving hot code up the tiers as it stays hot.

## Common misconceptions

- **"All browsers render the same."** They mostly do — but engine differences, especially around new CSS features and JS APIs, are real.
- **"Incognito mode hides me from websites."** It hides traces from your local device. The site, your ISP, and any third-party trackers can still see you.
