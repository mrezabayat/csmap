## In simple terms

A CPU could finish one instruction completely before starting the next, but most of its hardware would sit idle most of the time. A **pipeline** splits each instruction into a sequence of stages (fetch, decode, execute, write back) and feeds new instructions in continuously, so different parts of the CPU are busy on different instructions simultaneously — like an assembly line.

## More detail

The classic five-stage RISC pipeline:

1. **IF** — Instruction Fetch (read from memory).
2. **ID** — Instruction Decode (figure out what it is, read registers).
3. **EX** — Execute (the ALU does the work).
4. **MEM** — Memory access (load/store).
5. **WB** — Write Back (store the result in a register).

With a 5-stage pipeline, the CPU finishes (in steady state) one instruction per cycle, not one per five cycles. Real CPUs have 10–20 stages.

The catch is **hazards**:

- **Data hazard** — instruction B needs the result of instruction A that's still in the pipeline. Solved by **forwarding** (route the result early) or **stalling** (insert bubbles).
- **Control hazard** — a branch instruction's outcome is known only after EX, but IF has already loaded the next instruction. Solved by **branch prediction**: guess, speculate, and rewind on a misprediction.
- **Structural hazard** — two stages want the same hardware resource. Solved by duplicating the resource.

Modern CPUs add **superscalar** (multiple pipelines in parallel), **out-of-order execution** (reorder around stalls), and **simultaneous multithreading** (interleave two threads' instructions to keep stages fed).

## Why it matters

Pipelining is the technique that lets a 3 GHz CPU actually do a few billion operations per second instead of dropping by a factor of 5 to 20. It is the foundation of every other architectural trick in a modern processor.

## Real-world examples

- A modern x86 CPU has ~14–16 pipeline stages.
- A branch misprediction on a deeply pipelined CPU can cost ~15 cycles — the entire pipeline has to be flushed.
- Spectre and similar attacks abuse the speculative execution that pipelines need.

- Spectre and Meltdown (2018) were attacks against the speculative execution that pipelines depend on — patches cost up to 30% of throughput in some workloads.

## Common misconceptions

- **"Longer pipelines are always faster."** Longer pipelines allow higher clock speeds but pay more on every misprediction. The trade-off shifted away from very deep pipelines (Pentium 4 era) toward moderately deep, wider designs.
- **"Pipelining is free."** It is free *only* when there are no hazards. Real workloads hit hazards constantly; the entire job of an out-of-order core is to hide them.
